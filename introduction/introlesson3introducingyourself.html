Malagasy–Tsimihety Lesson 3: Introducing Yourself

(Appendix: basic grammar rules) • December 2022

It is important to know how to introduce yourself when you arrive in Madagascar. Let us see how it is done in Mandritsara:

One can talk about anything, but in practice, there are some things that are essential, like saying your name, where you are from and what you do for a living.

Basic Introduction

Anarako ……… (name) …….
Avy any ……… (place of origin) …… zaho
Asako ……… (job) ………… OR ……… (job) …… zaho

You might get a reply that sounds like the following:

Tonga soa eto ……… (insert destination)
Faly mahafantatra anao
Faly mandray anao

Word order note

Note that in official Malagasy, saying “my name is …” and “my job is …” are inverted.

Anarako … (name) … (Tsm)
vs … (name) … no anarako (Off)
Asako … (job) … (Tsm)
vs … (job) … no asako (Off)

After this obligatory introduction, you can talk about anything you like. For example, you can talk about your family or your spouse and children.

Manambady zaho — I am married.
TSY MANAMBADY ZAHO — I am not married.
MANANJANAKA ZAHO — I have children.

If you have lots of children, you can then elaborate by saying how many sons and daughters you have.

… (number) LALAHY … (number) VAIAVY
The word for boy is lehilahyoff/lalahytsm, and girl is vehivavyoff/vaiavytsm. In Malagasy, the word for boy/man/male are the same; likewise for girl/woman/female.

Cultural note

Malagasy society generally favours marriage and having children. If you are in neither of these categories, it might be difficult to avoid feeling a bit second-class. So, if you don’t know someone that well, it’s probably not a good idea to ask if they are married or how many children they have!

Two words for “children”

There are two words for children. If you are referring to children in relation to their parents, use zanaka. Otherwise, use zaza for children in the general sense.

Appendix: Basic Grammar Rules

  1. The subject of the sentence is at the end instead of the beginning.
I am from England.
Avy any Angletera zaho.

Note how the subject moves to the end of the sentence in Malagasy.

  1. “to be” or “to have” does not exist in the Malagasy language.

In English, the auxiliary am links subject and predicate, but in Malagasy, there is no equivalent; the sentence flows directly: Avy any Angletera zaho.

  1. Nouns do not have singular/plural forms.

The word Mananjanaka (to have children) stays the same; ray (one) or roa (two) indicates quantity.

  1. Similar to English, and unlike French, grammatical gender does not exist — no struggling with masculine and feminine forms!

Nouns and adjectives do not change for masculine/feminine. You won’t need separate forms.

  1. Malagasy uses possessive suffixes. When connected to a noun or adjective, they give a sense of possession.

  1. Unlike French, there are no complex verb conjugations in Malagasy — no être and avoir paradigms to memorize.

Verb forms are simple; tense markers are prefixes on the verb (see Rule 7).

  1. Like English, there are verb tenses, but it’s much simpler. In Malagasy, most verbs begin with the letter “m”. For past tense, “m” → “n”. For future tense, “m” → “h”.
