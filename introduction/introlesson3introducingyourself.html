Basic Introduction

In practice, three things are essential: your name, where you're from, and what you do.

Anarako … (name) … tsm vs … (name) … no anarako off
Avy any … (place) … zaho
Asako … (job) … tsm vs … (job) … no asako off

Tip

Tonga soa eto … — “Welcome to … (place) …”

Faly mahafantatra anao / Faly mandray anao — warm alternatives to greet someone after introductions.

Talking About Your Family

After the obligatory introduction, talk about your family, spouse, and children.

  • Manambady zaho — I am married.
  • Tsy manambady zaho — I am not married.
  • Mananjanaka zaho — I have children.
  • Mananjanaka ray — I have one child. / Mananjanaka roa — I have two children.
  • … lalahytsm / lehilahyoff — boy/man/male.
  • … vaiavytsm / vehivavyoff — girl/woman/female.

Cultural note

Malagasy society generally favours marriage and having children. If you don’t know someone well, avoid asking if they are married or how many children they have.

Grammar tip

tsy = not; tsisy = nothing. They are not the same.

Make negatives by adding tsy at the start: tsy miasa (not working).

Appendix: Basic Grammar Rules

  1. The subject of the sentence is often at the end.
  2. “To be” or “to have” do not exist as separate verbs.
  3. Nouns don’t change for singular/plural.
  4. No grammatical gender differences (unlike French).
  5. Possessive suffixes attach to nouns/adjectives.
  6. Most verbs begin with m-; past replaces m→n; future replaces m→h.
  7. tsy + verb/adj (not): tsy miasa. tsisy + noun (no): tsisy asa.

Tense pattern (example verb: miasa — work)

  • Present: Miasa zaho — I am working.
  • Past: Niasa zaho — I worked.
  • Future: Hiasa zaho — I will be working.
