Malagasy–Tsimihety Lesson 4: Going to the Market

(Appendix: numbers, money and groceries) • December 2022

Now that you have gained some basic expressions and grammar, this lesson will help you get ready to take on the local market and hopefully do your first shop!

Manao bazary / Mandeha bazary — “shopping for groceries” / “going to the market”.

Units of measurement at the market

  1. Individual units
  2. Pile = toko
  3. Kilo = kilao
  4. Litre = litatra
  5. Cups/tins = kapoaka

Money (Vola)

  • 1 Ar = 1 ariary
  • Ariary × 5 = Malagasy franc

When talking about money, ariary is used with 10 Ar (ariary folo) and 100 Ar (ariary zato) only. For other values the word is dropped: e.g. 200 Ar = roanjato.

In the market, prices generally start from about 100 Ar.

1. At the shops

Customer: Haody (you say it if you don’t see the shopkeeper)
Shopkeeper: Mandroso
Customer: Salama tompoko
Shopkeeper: Salama tompoko
Customer: Misy atody ve? (are there any eggs?)
Shopkeeper: Ia, misy. (yes, there are)
Customer: Ohatrino(na)? (how much are they?)
Shopkeeper: Fitonjato (700)
Customer: Amezotsm/Omeooff telo (3) azafady (give me 3 please)

ve — the question particle

Misy …… ve? = “is there … ?”. ve is an interrogative particle that’s very useful. Add it to a closed question to elicit a yes/no answer. Think of it as replacing the rising intonation in English.

“Mandroso” at the market

Even though there is no physical shop, mandroso is used to invite the customer to approach and make a purchase.

Vocabulary:
Atody = eggs • Mandroso = come and buy • Telo kapoaka = 3 cups • Telo toko = 3 piles • Tsisy madinikaoff/la monnaietsm = no change • Vary = rice • Ronono = milk • Tomaty/voatabia = tomatoes • Siramamy = sugar

2. At the market

Vendor: Mandroso
Customer: Ohatrino vary? (vary = rice)
Vendor: 700 Ar
Customer: Amezo telo kapoaka azafady.
Vendor: Azafady, tsisy la monnaie / tsisy madinika. (madinika = change)

Examples with quantities:

  • 3 eggs = atody telo
  • 3 piles of tomatoes = tomaty/voatabia 3 toko
  • 3 kilos of sugar = siramamy 3 kilao
  • 3 litres of milk = ronono 3 litatra
  • 3 cups of rice = vary 3 kapoaka

Francs vs Ariary

If the price is not displayed, do ask. The price is usually quoted in ariary. The Malagasy franc was replaced by ariary in 2005 at a rate of 5 francs = 1 Ar. You may still hear vendors quote in francs, especially to foreigners—always confirm the unit and know your ×5 table!

Appendix 1 — Numbers / Counting

NY ISA = numbers; MANISA = to count.

  • Rule 1: Malagasy numbers are read from right to left.
  • Rule 2: The English “and” is replaced with:
    • ambin’ny — for 11 to 19
    • amby — for 21 to 199
    • sy — for numbers beyond 200

Audio walks the table from right (units) to left (thousands/millions).

  • 11 iraika ambin’ny folo
  • 17 fito ambin’ny folo
  • 19 sivy ambin’ny folo
  • 21 iraika amby roapolo
  • 85 dimy amby valopolo

Appendix 2 — Common market items (exercise)

Try saying these in Malagasy.

  1. How much is the rice?
  2. How much are the big onions? (note: big = be. Adjectives follow the noun.)
  3. How much is the flour?
  4. How much is the oil?
  5. How much is the chicken?

Misy … ve?

  1. Is there any sugar?
  2. Is there any milk?
  3. Are there any eggs?
  4. No meat
  5. No bread

Amezotsm/Omeooff …

  1. Give me 2 eggs please.
  2. Give me 2 big onions please.
  3. Give me 2 kilos of salt please.
  4. Give me 2 litres of oil please.
  5. Give me 2 cups of rice please.
  6. 1 kilo of meat
  7. 4 bananas
  8. 3 piles of tomatoes
  9. 5 tins of rice
  10. How much are the bananas? 200 Ar, or 3 for 500 Ar.

Quick market phrases

  • Tsara ve? — Is it good/fresh?
  • Asehoy kely azafady. — Please show me.
  • Apesa kely. — Add a little (top up).
  • Ataovy roa. — Make it two.
  • Misaotra, hiverina aho. — Thanks, I’ll come back.
