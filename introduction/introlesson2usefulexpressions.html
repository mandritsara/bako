Malagasy–Tsimihety Lesson 2: Useful Expressions

Now that you have learnt how to say “hello” and “goodbye”, here are other common words and phrases for daily use.

(Appendix: Malagasy Hospitality)

Thank you

Although misaotra is sufficient, saying misaotra betsaka is more polite.

Person 1: MISAOTRA — “thank you”
Person 2: TSISY FISAORANA — “not at all / you’re welcome”

Excuse me / Please / Pardon / Sorry

You will hear azafady used a lot. It’s a very useful word that can mean different things depending on the context:

  1. When walking past someone, say azafady.
  2. To apologise for a mistake: azafady (or see below for stronger apologies).
  3. Like “excuse me” at the start of a sentence: Azafady, ...
Person 1 (walking): AZAFADY
Person 2 (stationary): NDAO — “go ahead”
Person 1: AZAFADY
Person 2: TSY MANINONA — “no problem”

MIALA TSINY — another way to apologise for something trivial.

MIFONA — to ask forgiveness for a serious offence (also used in the Bible).

Body language

  • When walking past people, slightly bow your body and stretch your right hand out.
  • When giving or receiving a gift, stretch out both hands to express politeness.
  • In Mandritsara, when one gives something or says misaotra, one often bends the knee slightly as a sign of reverence.

Bon voyage

To wish someone a safe journey, say soava dia.

Person 1: SOAVA DIA
Person 2: MISAOTRA BETSAKA

Welcome

To say “welcome,” combine “arrived” and “well” — Tonga Soa.

Person 1: TONGA SOA
Person 2: MISAOTRA BETSAKA

Have a good day / Keep it up

Mazotoa is another versatile word, a bit like azafady. It can mean “enjoy,” “have a good day,” “keep it up,” etc.

Person 1: MAZOTOA
Person 2: MISAOTRA, IANAO KOA — “thank you, you too”

Come in / Join me

You will have encountered mandroso in Lesson 1a (what you say to a guest when they arrive). If you are having a meal or a drink and someone walks past, you can also say mandroso as a polite invitation to join you. The person is not obliged to sit and eat, and can simply reply, Misaotra, mazotoa.

Appendix: Malagasy Hospitality

There is a Malagasy proverb, Valala iray ifanapahana, literally “we can share a cricket between us.” It expresses the joy of sharing food.

Another proverb is Hani-masaka tsisy tompony — “the meal is ready for everyone.” Imagine you’re eating with family and guests arrive unexpectedly. Even if you haven’t prepared enough, the idea is to share with everyone regardless of quantity.

Hospitality is central to Malagasy culture and often surpasses visitors’ expectations. Practices vary by context, but in many villages:

  • If the host has just finished a meal and visitors arrive, the host will still cook for them.
  • If the host is still eating when visitors arrive, they will share their meal.
  • If visitors try to leave before the meal is ready, they are told to wait for the meal: Miandrasa sakafooff or Mandesa hagninytsm.

Note: Usage is more common in villages and is declining in cities and among younger people, but it’s helpful to understand the strong emphasis on food and sharing in Malagasy culture.
