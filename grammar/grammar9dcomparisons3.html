Malagasy–Tsimihety Grammar Lesson 9d

Comparisons (III)

March 2025 — Malagasy–Tsimihety with Bako

1. Comparatives and reduplication

In Malagasy, reduplication can soften or intensify the meaning of an adjective.

If the word has a negative meaning, it becomes more positive when duplicated, and vice versa.

Examples:

ratsy (bad) → ratsiratsy (less bad)
mafana (hot) → mafanafana (less hot) 

(-) ---- ratsy / manintsy ---- ratsiratsy / manintsinintsy ---- tsara / mafana ----> (+)

However, comparatives in the Tsimihety dialect don’t always follow the same change in meaning as the duplication would suggest.

2. Using comparatives across a scale

Some adjectives naturally sit on a spectrum rather than a simple good / bad opposition.

The comparative form may indicate movement along that scale rather than a clear improvement or deterioration.

3. Faran’ny … = “The most extreme of …”

Faran’ny is used to express the superlative: the most / the -est.

Examples:

Faran’ny sarotra ny malagasy
→ Malagasy is the most difficult language

Faran’ny miasa ny vitsika
→ Ants are the hardest working

Faran’ny manintsy i Rosia
→ Russia is the coldest

Faran’ny matsiro ny hena omby
→ Beef is the most delicious
